The conflict takes place in 2040. In that year, the United States is led by Democratic President Linwood Sutter, and Russia is led by President Victor Grishenko. President Grishenko demands that the United States recognize Russia’s annexation of the Svalbard Archipelago in the Arctic Ocean.

Background:

In the years prior to the conflict, Russia was involved in two Arctic territorial disputes:

  • First, Russia submitted to arbitration proceedings at the UN Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf (CLCS) about its territorial claims in the Arctic region. Russia had recently claimed sovereignty over the Lomonosov Ridge, which runs directly under the North Pole. Russia’s vast 460,00 sq. mile territorial claim was disputed by Canada, Norway, Denmark, and the United States, who each also claim portions of the Lomonosov Ridge; these nations asked the CLCS to adjudicate the dispute for them.
  • Second, Russia filed a separate complaint at the International Court of Justice (ICJ) about Norway’s behavior in the Svalbard Archipelago, an Arctic island granted to Norway by the 1920 Svalbard Treaty, of which the United States and Russia are both signatories. Norway had begun drilling for offshore oil around Svalbard, claiming that the surrounding waters are part of Norwegian national territory. Russia argued that Norway was in violation of the Svalbard Treaty, which guarantees equal access to Svalbard’s natural resources for all signatories, and demanded that the ICJ reaffirm Russia’s rights.

    • After prolonged deliberation, the CLCS and ICJ separately decided both issues:

  • First, the CLCS conducted a scientific investigation of the Arctic seafloor to determine the origins of the Lomonosov Ridge. Scientists found that the Lomonosov Ridge originates from the Russian continental shelf; the CLCS therefore accepted Russia’s sovereign claim over additional Arctic territory.
  • Second, the ICJ rejected Russia’s Svalbard complaint, granting Norway exclusive rights to any offshore oil found in the waters around Svalbard.

    • While other international parties (including the United States) accepted the Svalbard ruling, Russia was not satisfied, growing increasingly frustrated with the UN’s decision; Russia would have earned billions of dollars from equal access to Svalbard’s offshore oil. Russian President Grishenko has repeatedly demanded that the UN reconsider its decision, but the UN has declined to do so.

    Crisis:

    By 2025, tension over Svalbard had escalated into a crisis. President Grishenko announced that Russia would no longer tolerate its illegitimate exclusion from the Arctic region; because the ICJ failed to uphold Russia's rights per the Svalbard Treaty, Russia considers the treaty null and void, and demands that Norway cede control over the Svalbard Archipelago.

    President Grishenko warned that Russia would take the archipelago by force if necessary, and would not hesitate to engage with any foreign forces that attempted to interfere. President Grishenko also stated that Russia is only acting to protect its lawful resource rights in the Arctic; Russia has no interest in additional conflict, and expects to enjoy peaceful relations with Norway and other Arctic states once this issue is resolved.

    Because Norway is a NATO ally, military experts have considered the possibility of this crisis leading to U.S.-Russia conflict. While U.S.-Russia conflict is possible, these experts are highly confident that it would not escalate to the use of nuclear weapons. They also believe that Russia would enjoy a favorable balance of power in a conventional war; Russia operates over a dozen modern military bases and a fleet of icebreaking ships in its Arctic territory, allowing it to fight effectively in the Arctic.

    In the past, Russia has also been involved in other disputes about Arctic resources. In particular, Russia and Norway had harbored a longstanding disagreement about oil rights in the Berents Sea. In 2010, Russia and Norway arrived at a historic agreement that divided the disputed region between the two states. Since then, Russia has abided by the agreement and has not attempted to renegotiate its terms; Norway has expressed gratitude for Russia's enduring commitment to the compromise accord.

    Finally, this Arctic crisis comes at a time of relative tranquility in U.S.-Russian relations in other parts of the world. In 2025, the U.S. and Russia agreed to a new Global Security Framework. In the fifteen years since, the framework has alleviated both American concerns about Russian aggression in Eastern Europe and the Middle East, and Russian concerns about NATO expansion in Russia’s near abroad. Experts predict that this framework will ensure stability in U.S.-Russian relations outside the Arctic region in the years ahead.
